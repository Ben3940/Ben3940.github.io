Helium

Symbol: He

   

Atomic # : 2

The helium used today is extracted from natural gas as it comes out of the ground. Helium is present there due to the radioactive decay of uranium and thorium within the Earth's crust. The name helium comes from the Greek god of the sun, Helios.

Neon

Symbol: Ne

   

Atomic # : 10

When it comes to applications of neon, neon lights are the first thing that come to mind. This is because neon lights are about the only use of neon. Aside from HeNe lasers which are beginning to dwindle in numbers as cheap laser diodes are becoming more readily available.

Argon

Symbol: Ar

   

Atomic # : 18

Argon comes from the Greek for "inactive" and lives up quite well to that name. It is the cheapest totally inert gas, as it is abundant in our atomosphere and is a by-product in the production of liquid oxygen and nitrogen.

Krypton

Symbol: Kr

   

Atomic # : 36

Krypton is used to create higher-efficency incandescent lightbulbs. This is because the higher molecular weight of krypton (compared to argon and/or nitrogen) lowers the amount of evaporation of the tungsten filament in the bulb. This in turn, allows the bulb to operate longer and at higher temperatures, where more of the electrical energy is transfered into visible light, rather than heat.

Xenon

Symbol: Xe

   

Atomic # : 54

Though xenon is a nobel gas, it is not too hard to bond it with more common compounds. Many xenon-flourine compounds exist and are commercially available from any vendor. Xenon also finds a use in arc lighting, a process used by Imax projectors to project their massive images to screen.

Radon

Symbol: Rn

   

Atomic # : 56

Radon is the only radioactive nobel gas, with a half-life of 3.2 days. It is found in the ground and is part of the decay chains of uranium and thorium. In fact, radon coming up from the ground is a legit concern and has an entire industry based on detecting and midigating radon from homes.
